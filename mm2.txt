The rain hadn't stopped in three days.
São Paulo's industrial district looked like it was drowning from the inside out—rusted scaffolding bleeding orange streaks down crumbling concrete, gutters overflowing with black water that smelled like oil and rot. The kind of place where the city forgot to send cleanup crews because nobody important lived there anymore.
Isaac Moreira stood under a half-collapsed overpass, watching the rain hammer the broken asphalt. His reflection rippled in a puddle at his feet—distorted, flickering, wrong. Half his face looked human. The other half looked like someone had taken glass and tried to reshape it into skin.
He'd stopped looking in mirrors two years ago.
His coat hung loose around his shoulders, heavy with rain, hiding the white fractures that ran along his arms like cracks in porcelain. They glowed faintly in the dark—rift burn, they called it. The scar tissue left behind when reality tried to reject you and failed.
He flexed his fingers. The glow pulsed brighter for a second, then dimmed.
"Still here," he muttered to himself. "Still changing."
The rain answered with nothing but noise.

Five Years Earlier – Metro Collapse
Isaac had been twenty-three when the world broke open.
Back then, he worked as a recovery medic for the city's emergency response unit—the team they sent into disaster zones when everyone else was running out. It wasn't glamorous work. Mostly pulling bodies from rubble, stabilizing trauma victims, trying not to think too hard about the ones you couldn't save.
He'd been good at it. Focused. The kind of person who could compartmentalize grief into a box labeled "later" and keep moving.
Then came the metro tunnel incident.
The call came in at 4:47 AM. Partial collapse near Linha Vermelha station. Cause unknown. Estimated thirty civilians trapped. Standard protocol: secure the site, extract survivors, contain structural damage.
What they didn't tell them—what nobody knew yet—was that it wasn't a collapse.
It was a Rift Event.
Isaac's team arrived eighteen minutes after the initial report. The tunnel entrance looked normal at first—dust clouds, scattered debris, emergency lights strobing through the haze. Then they went deeper.
The temperature dropped fifteen degrees in the span of ten meters.
The air tasted metallic, electric, like ozone before a lightning strike.
And the walls… the walls were breathing.
"Command, we've got visual anomalies," Isaac's team leader radioed. "Structural integrity compromised but—Christ, the metal's moving—"
Static swallowed the transmission.
Isaac pushed forward with three others—Carla, Miguel, and a rookie whose name he never learned. They found the civilians huddled in a maintenance alcove, faces pale with shock. Some were crying. Others just stared at nothing, eyes glassy and distant.
"We're getting you out," Isaac said, keeping his voice steady. "Stay close, stay calm."
That's when the tunnel screamed.
Not a sound. A sensation—like reality itself was tearing, a pressure wave that hit his chest and squeezed the air from his lungs. The walls buckled inward. Concrete twisted like wet clay. And in the center of the tunnel, space folded.
It looked like someone had punched a hole through the world and left the wound open.
The Rift.
Miguel grabbed Isaac's arm. "We need to move—NOW—"
But it was already too late.
The distortion expanded—a silent implosion of light and shadow that swallowed sound itself. Isaac saw Carla reach for a civilian. Saw her fingers brush the woman's shoulder. Saw her disintegrate—not explode, not burn, just cease—particles scattering like dust in wind.
Miguel screamed. The rookie ran. Isaac stood frozen, watching his team vanish one by one into the distortion's pull.
He should have run.
He should have died.
Instead, something in him snapped.
"NO!"
He lunged forward, grabbed two civilians by their jackets, and pulled—hauling them backward with strength he didn't know he had. His vision whited out. His body felt like it was being pulled in opposite directions. Pain lanced through every nerve ending, hot and sharp and wrong.
Then the Rift closed.
The tunnel collapsed.
Everything went dark.

Three Days Later – Hospital
Isaac woke up in a sterile white room with machines beeping around him and tubes running into his arms.
His body hurt in ways that didn't make sense. Not broken bones or torn muscle—something deeper. Like his cells were vibrating at the wrong frequency.
A nurse came in, saw he was awake, and immediately left to get a doctor.
Isaac tried to sit up. His hands looked… normal. Mostly. There were faint white lines running along his forearms, barely visible against his skin. He touched one. It was warm.
The doctor arrived—middle-aged woman with tired eyes and a clipboard. "Señor Moreira. You're lucky to be alive."
"My team—"
"I'm sorry." Her voice was gentle but final. "You were the only one we pulled out."
Isaac's throat closed.
"You've been unconscious for three days," the doctor continued. "We ran every test we could. Physically, you're stable. But…" She hesitated. "There are some anomalies we can't explain. Unusual tissue scarring. Elevated electromagnetic readings. We'd like to keep you for observation—"
"I'm fine," Isaac said. His voice sounded hollow.
"Señor Moreira—"
"I'm fine."
He wasn't.

The First Signs
It started small.
Two days after discharge, Isaac dropped a glass in his kitchen. It shattered on impact—normal. But the shards didn't scatter. They hovered for half a second, suspended in midair, before clattering to the floor.
He stared at them, heart pounding.
Coincidence. Had to be.
That night, he dreamed of the tunnel. Carla's face dissolving. Miguel's scream cutting off mid-breath. The Rift folding space like origami.
He woke up to find every window in his apartment cracked in perfect radial patterns.
The third incident happened during a grocery run. A kid knocked over a display rack. Cans tumbled toward Isaac. He flinched—and they stopped. Hung in the air for three seconds. Then dropped.
An old woman nearby crossed herself and hurried away.
Isaac left without buying anything.
That night, his reflection started whispering.
He was brushing his teeth when he saw it—his reflection's mouth moving out of sync with his own. Forming words he couldn't hear. He leaned closer. The reflection smiled.
Then spoke in a voice that wasn't his:
"You didn't die. Why didn't you die?"
Isaac punched the mirror. Blood and glass. His hand healed in thirty seconds.
He knew then.
He wasn't human anymore.

The Transformation
The next six months were a slow-motion nightmare.
His body changed incrementally—skin developing faint fractures of light, eyes sometimes flickering white when he was stressed, shadows clinging to him longer than they should. He stopped going outside during the day. Stopped answering calls from his few remaining friends.
He researched obsessively—every scrap of information about the metro collapse, about "Rift Events," about the things that came back from them. The internet had fragments. Conspiracy forums. Blurry photos. Witness testimonies dismissed as mass hysteria.
And one word that kept appearing:
Rifter.
Humans transformed by Rift exposure. Most went feral within weeks—mindless, aggressive, hunted down by government response teams. A few retained some cognitive function but were still considered threats. Detained. Studied. Disappeared.
Isaac looked at his hands—the white fractures glowing faintly in the dark.
He was becoming one of them.
He had two choices: turn himself in and become a lab experiment, or disappear.
He chose the latter.

The Undercity
São Paulo's lower industrial zones were a patchwork of abandoned warehouses, condemned apartment blocks, and infrastructure the city had given up on. The kind of place where people minded their own business because survival depended on not asking questions.
Isaac made it his territory.
He learned his abilities through trial and error—how to phase-shift through solid matter for brief moments, how to "imprint" emotions onto objects and temporarily alter their properties, how to sense other distortions in reality before they fully manifested.
He wasn't strong. Not like the feral Rifters he sometimes encountered—the ones who'd lost themselves completely, reduced to pure instinct and hunger. But he was smart. Strategic. He avoided detection, moved through shadows, and tried desperately to hold onto the parts of himself that still felt human.
The hardest part wasn't the physical changes.
It was the loneliness.
He'd spent his whole life around people—working with teams, saving lives, being part of something larger than himself. Now every human interaction was a risk. One slip, one moment of lost control, and he'd expose himself.
So he stopped trying.
He became a ghost in his own city.

The Others
Over the years, Isaac encountered seven other Rifters.
Tokyo, Japan – A former salaryman who could warp sound into physical weapons. Isaac had been tracking international reports, trying to understand the global scope of Rift Events, when he found footage of a sonic-based attack in Shinjuku. The Rifter had gone completely feral—no speech, no recognition, just raw destructive instinct. Japanese response teams took him down with specialized equipment. Isaac watched the video three times, stomach churning. That's my future.
Madrid, Spain – This one was different. The Rifter had learned to vanish completely, leaving only moving shadows that mimicked human voices. Isaac tracked her through underground forums—rumors of a "ghost" in the Madrid metro system, helping stranded travelers find their way out during blackouts but never showing her face. He tried to make contact. Left messages in places only someone phasing through walls could find. She never responded. Two months later, the sightings stopped. Isaac didn't know if she'd moved on or been captured.
Osaka, Japan – Reports of a Rifter who could distort time in a small radius. Witnesses described him as a middle-aged man who moved in "stuttered frames," appearing ten feet away from where he'd been a second earlier. He'd been protecting a children's hospital during a Rift surge, keeping collapsing debris frozen in mid-air long enough for evacuation. Then he collapsed. Isaac found documentation of his detention—classified, but he'd learned how to access certain databases. The man's name was Takeshi Yamada. Status: contained, cognitive function degrading. Isaac had stared at that file for hours.
The others were similar stories. Fragments of humanity clinging to distorted bodies. Some trying to help. Some just trying to survive. None of them lasting long.
Isaac kept a journal—handwritten, hidden in a waterproof container buried beneath his makeshift shelter. Names. Dates. Abilities. Outcomes. A record of people the world wanted to forget.
At the bottom of the last page, he'd written in shaky handwriting:
"We weren't monsters. We were people who refused to die."

Present Day – The Rescue
Isaac stood in the rain, watching the city lights flicker across the skyline. Another Rift pulse had rippled through the district three hours ago—minor, barely registering on official sensors, but he'd felt it like a migraine spike.
Rift Events were getting more frequent. Closer together. Stronger.
Something was changing.
He heard the screams before he saw the smoke.
Two blocks east—a residential building, half-collapsed, flames licking through broken windows. Civilians trapped inside. Emergency services were already en route, sirens wailing in the distance, but they wouldn't arrive in time.
Isaac's first instinct was to walk away.
Getting involved meant exposure. Meant risk. Meant possibly ending up like Takeshi Yamada—detained, studied, forgotten.
But he heard a child crying.
"Dammit," he muttered.
He ran.
The building's entrance was blocked by debris. Isaac phased through—his body flickering translucent for two seconds as he passed through concrete and rebar. The strain pulled at something deep in his chest, but he pushed through.
Inside was chaos. Smoke, heat, collapsing structural beams. A family huddled in a corner—mother, father, two kids. The father had a broken leg. The mother was trying to shield her children from falling debris.
Isaac's hands moved on instinct—medic training overriding Rifter instinct. He assessed injuries, calculated weight distribution, planned an extraction route.
"Can you walk?" he asked the father.
The man stared at him—at the fractures of light glowing along Isaac's exposed skin—and his face went pale.
"You're—you're one of them—"
"Yeah," Isaac said. "And I'm getting you out. Now move."
He lifted a fallen beam with one hand—impossible strength, matter responding to his imprinted command—and created a path. The family stumbled through. Isaac guided them to a service exit, phasing through locked doors, keeping them ahead of the spreading flames.
They made it outside just as fire trucks arrived.
